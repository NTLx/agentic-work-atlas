# The Last Technical Interview

**Author**: Steve Yegge
**Source**: Medium
**Published**: May 29, 2026
**Read Time**: 18 minutes

## Introduction

Yegge declares the technical interview process is "on its last leg" after 35 years of conducting them. He notes the system has been broken since before he started and despite band-aid attempts, remains fundamentally flawed.

## Two Big Dogs

Amazon's Bar Raisers and Microsoft's As-Appropriate roles exist because "you can't trust your interview teams to make good hiring decisions." Yegge was part of the core group defining the Bar Raiser role. Despite various fixes, companies still "hire tons of false positives" and reject qualified candidates.

## Remember That Time We All Fired Ourselves?

Google's Hiring Committee reviewed anonymized packets and voted against hiring about two-thirds. The twist: "those were our own packets from when we had all interviewed at Google." The exercise revealed "how utterly broken our process was" but nothing changed.

## Change is Taboo

Questioning interview validity is like "farting in church." The fundamental process of short interviews "hasn't changed in fifty years." Yegge predicts technical interviews will fade "into a cute historical footnote like phrenology."

## The Signal Problem

Résumés have poor signal-to-noise ratio, worsened by AI writing. Phone screens were better but obsolete. On-site interviews are "infamously unlike real-world work." The standard process collects only "a few hours of signal" for decisions lasting years.

## Provisional Employment to the Rescue

Internships provide much stronger signals than interview loops. Yegge's first employer, Geoworks, required six-month co-ops, producing "a company of nearly all stars and superstars." Amazon later acqui-hired this group, with leaders calling them "by far the strongest group of people they ever brought in."

## You Call This a Rescue?

Senior engineers historically refused provisional roles, but market changes may shift this equilibrium. The system faces breakdowns: "offline assessments poisoned by AI cheating" and rapid changes in job roles making traditional evaluation obsolete.

## Let's Stop Simulating Work

Interview steps are "elaborate attempts to simulate actually working with someone" through theater like whiteboards and made-up questions. The solution: "stop simulating" and use real work as assessment.

## Campfire Is The New Interview

The new trend in SF is "come work with us for a few days" with paid, real work on actual codebases. Yegge calls this the "campfire" model: "pull up a log, build something together, see how it feels."

## Count Work Twice

Work should count for companies as assessment signal and real output, while candidates receive "a permanent, portable record of what they did and how well they did it, signed by you, whether or not you make an offer."

## Make Interviewing A Profit Center

Campfire-style hiring flips interviewing from cost center to profit center, paying for itself through shipped work and building "gravity" that attracts strong candidates.

## Where Do the Stamps Originate?

Like rideshare platforms with ratings, the key is "letting the workers carry the assessments out the door." Yegge acknowledges solutions are evolving but wanted to "move the Overton window" on fixing assessment "properly for the first time in 50 years."
